# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Albion Codex V2 is used by the project owner and a small group of trusted friends who already understand Albion Online market trading. They are not onboarding into the game or learning basic economics; they are trying to make fast, low-friction decisions while comparing real market orders from Caerleon and the Black Market.

The user context is practical and time-sensitive: the player has the game, local API, web dashboard, and Albion Data Client running on the same machine, and wants to act before opportunities go stale.

## Product Purpose

The product exists to isolate one profitable mechanic: buy sell offers in the normal Caerleon market and immediately sell into the best Black Market buy orders. Success means surfacing opportunities that are fresh, quantity-aware, and easy to trust, then helping the user decide whether to save, hide, or mark an offer as executed.

The interface should make the best actions obvious without pretending the data is more certain than it is. Private captured orders, public market snapshots, stale data, missing quantities, fees, margin, and execution status all matter because they change whether an opportunity is real enough to act on.

## Brand Personality

Technical, fast, and premium.

The product voice should feel direct, competent, and focused. It can use trading and game-market language naturally, but it should avoid hype. The ideal feeling is a sharp private tool built for people who know what they are doing: confident enough to be quick, precise enough to avoid bad trades.

## Anti-references

Avoid generic SaaS dashboards with interchangeable cards, vague productivity language, and decorative metrics.

Avoid crypto trading terminals: no neon chaos, no performative finance aesthetic, no fake institutional seriousness, no overwhelming chart-wall energy.

Avoid ugly spreadsheets: dense information is welcome, but the surface should not feel like a raw table dump, default admin template, or patched-together data grid.

Avoid game-fantasy ornament unless it directly helps item recognition or Albion context. The product should be Albion-aware, not cosplay.

## Design Principles

1. Prioritize actionable certainty: make freshness, quantity confidence, data source, and fees visible wherever they affect the trade decision.

2. Optimize for fast scanning: the user should be able to compare opportunities, filter noise, and choose an action without reading explanatory copy.

3. Preserve expert density with premium craft: keep the information rich, but use spacing, hierarchy, and interaction polish so it feels deliberate rather than cramped.

4. Treat risk as first-class information: stale prices, estimated quantities, private/public source differences, and failed refreshes should be clear without derailing the workflow.

5. Keep the mechanic narrow: every screen should serve the Caerleon to Black Market flip loop unless a new feature earns its place.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

No specific accessibility requirements have been defined for the current private-user audience.

Future UI work should still preserve sensible defaults: readable contrast, keyboard-reachable controls, clear focus states, reduced reliance on color alone for status, and motion that remains subtle enough not to distract during quick trading decisions.
